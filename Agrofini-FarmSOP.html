Section 02 — Universal Protocols

Farm Management Fundamentals — Rules That Apply to Every Crop

These protocols must be in place before the first seed goes in the ground.

2.1 Soil Preparation Standard

Step 1 — Soil Test (Week 0)

Send soil samples to IITA Ibadan (Oyo Road) or Ogun State Agricultural Development Programme (ADP) lab. Test for pH, nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), potassium (K), and organic matter. Cost: ₦10,000–₦20,000. This single test saves ₦500,000+ in wrong fertiliser decisions.

Target soil pH: 6.0–6.8 for all vegetables. Below 5.8 = apply agricultural lime (1–2 tonnes/ha) 2 weeks before planting.

Step 2 — Primary Tillage (Week 1)

Ploughing depth: 25–30cm. This is non-negotiable. Shallow ploughing (10–15cm) cripples root development and reduces yield by 25–40%. Hire a tractor — do not substitute with hand hoes for this step.

Plough 2–3 weeks before planting to allow weed seeds to germinate and die before crop establishment.

Step 3 — Bed Preparation (Week 1–2)

Harrow to break clods. Form raised beds 15–20cm high for all crops (prevents waterlogging). Beds: 1.0–1.2m wide with 40–50cm working paths between. Raised beds are especially critical for pepper, tomatoes, and bell pepper which are susceptible to root rot in standing water.

2.4 Integrated Pest Management (IPM) — Universal Protocol

Prevention First

  • Scout all zones twice weekly — early morning, walk every bed. Look under leaves. Catch pest outbreaks at 5 insects, not 500.
  • Remove and burn (do not compost) any diseased plants immediately. Do not carry infected plant material through clean zones.
  • Rotate crops — never plant the same crop family in the same bed two consecutive seasons. This breaks pest and disease cycles more effectively than any chemical.
  • Maintain clean farm paths — overgrown weeds on paths are pest refuges that reinvade crops at night.
  • Quarantine all new planting material (setts, seedlings from outside) for 1 week before introducing to farm.

Spray Discipline

  • ALWAYS wear gloves, goggles, and a mask when mixing and applying. No exception. Never eat or touch your face during spraying.
  • Read every label. Mix only the prescribed rate — over-application burns plants and kills beneficial insects; under-application builds resistance.
  • Spray early morning (6–8am) or late afternoon (4–6pm) only. Never spray in full midday sun — chemicals degrade and plant tissue burns.
  • Do not spray within 7 days of harvest for most chemicals. Check label for Pre-Harvest Interval (PHI).
  • Rotate chemical classes — use different active ingredients in rotation to prevent resistance building.
  • Wash sprayer thoroughly after each chemical — residues react badly and damage next crop.

Land Preparation & Planting SOP

1

Prepare raised beds 20cm high, 1m wide. Cucumber roots do not tolerate waterlogging — even 12 hours of standing water kills the crop. Good drainage is essential.

2

Apply base fertiliser before planting: NPK 15-15-15 at 200 kg/ha (20g per sqm). Mix into top 10cm of bed soil at least 3 days before sowing.

3

Sow 2 seeds per hole, 2cm deep. Spacing: 60cm between plants, 100cm between rows. After germination (4–6 days), thin to 1 plant per hole — leave the strongest seedling.

4

Optional: trellis system. Single string or net trellis at 1.5m height increases air circulation, reduces disease pressure, and allows easier harvesting. Adds ₦30,000/1,000 sqm setup cost but yields improve 15–20%.

Nursery Protocol (Critical — Do Not Skip)

1

Nursery medium: Mix top soil : sand : compost in 1:1:1 ratio. Fill nursery trays or black polythene bags. Sow 2 seeds per cell, 0.5cm deep. Keep in shade net (50%).

2

Germination: 5–8 days at 25–30°C. Water twice daily with a watering can — never flood. If outdoor temperature drops below 18°C at night, cover nursery with clear polythene.

3

Hardening off (Week 3): Move seedlings from full shade to 30% shade for 5 days before transplanting. This acclimatises them to field conditions and reduces transplant shock by 60–70%.

4

Transplant at 4–5 leaf stage (25–30 days after sowing). Water nursery beds thoroughly 2 hours before transplanting — roots should come out intact.

Field Planting

5

Transplant in the afternoon or on a cloudy day — never midday sun. Transplant shock is the biggest killer of early-stage tomato crops.

6

Spacing: 50cm between plants × 60cm between rows = 33,000 plants/ha (3,300 plants per 1,000 sqm). Apply 1L water per plant immediately after transplanting.

7

Stake every plant with a 1.2m bamboo stake within 1 week of transplanting — before the plant needs it. Installing stakes after flowering disturbs roots.

Why Bell Pepper Needs Special Treatment

Bell pepper earns 3–5× the price of tatashe, but it requires more precise management and — critically — a pre-arranged buyer before a single seed is planted. It does not sell well at Mile 12 wholesale — it is a premium B2B product for hotels, supermarkets, and fast-food chains.

1

Nursery: 4 weeks. Bell pepper is more temperature-sensitive than tatashe — maintain 22–28°C for germination. Below 18°C: germination fails. Do not use rainy season for first cycle if you can't protect the nursery.

2

Season timing: Plant S1 in February (nursery January) for May–July harvest. Plant S2 in August (nursery July) for November–January harvest. Avoid planting when daytime temperatures exceed 35°C consistently — flower drop becomes excessive above this threshold.

3

Calcium is critical for bell pepper specifically. Blossom-end rot is the #1 quality problem. Apply calcium nitrate foliar every 7 days from first flower bud. Never let irrigation run dry — calcium uptake requires consistent soil moisture.

4

Colour development: Most bell pepper is harvested green. To achieve red/yellow/orange (premium price 30–40% higher), leave fruit on plant 3–4 additional weeks after green maturity. The plant needs extra potassium during this stage.

Quality Standards (B2B requirement)

  • Grade A (hotel/supermarket): Uniform size 150–200g, fully developed lobes (3–4), firm skin, no blemishes, smooth surface. 90% of Zone E harvest should meet this standard.
  • Grade B: Slightly irregular shape, minor cosmetic issues — sell to wholesale/market at standard pepper price.
  • Pack in single-layer ventilated trays or clamshell packs for hotel delivery. Never bag in nylon sacks.

Planting Protocol

1

Mound preparation: Raise mounds 30–40cm high, 60–80cm diameter. Space mounds 2m × 2m. Incorporate compost or aged manure into mound before planting — this single action increases yield by 40–60%. Compost: 5–10kg per mound.

3

Harvesting Protocol

H

H

Bundle into standard market bundles (approx 1kg fresh weight). Tie loosely — tight bundles generate heat and yellow the leaves within hours, reducing price.

H

Spray bundles lightly with water and wrap in banana leaves or damp hessian for transport. Shelf life is 24–36 hours maximum. Same-day or next-morning market delivery is essential.

H

Market: Lagos traders visit Ogun State farm areas weekly. Build relationships with 2–3 regular traders who buy directly at farm gate. This eliminates transport cost and ensures guaranteed weekly offtake.

💡 Extended Use: After 12–14 months, plants begin to decline. Before clearing, collect mature seeds from large fruits (1–2kg per large fruit). Seeds sell at ₦1,500–₦3,000/kg — a bonus harvest with zero additional input cost.

Planting & Management

1

Pre-treatment: Soak seeds in warm water for 12–24 hours before planting. This softens the seed coat and improves germination from 60–70% to 85–90%. Discard seeds that float — they are non-viable.

2

4

Planting & Key Agronomy Rules

1

Best season: dry (October–January planting). Watermelon planted in the wet season suffers from fungal diseases and fruit cracking. With irrigation, dry season watermelon reaches premium price (₦200–₦280/kg vs ₦100–₦150 in rainy season).

2

Spacing: 2m × 1.5m = 3,333 plants/ha. Watermelon vines spread aggressively — tight spacing = reduced fruit size and increased disease. Never be tempted to plant closer to "fit more plants in."

3

Pollination: Watermelon requires bee pollination. If few bees on farm: hand-pollinate by transferring pollen from male to female flowers with a small brush between 6–10am when flowers are open. Poor pollination = deformed, hollow fruit.

4

Fruit thinning: Leave only 2–3 fruits per vine. Remove excess fruits when they are tennis-ball size — concentrating plant energy into fewer fruits produces larger, sweeter, more marketable melons.

5

Fertility: High-potassium phase is critical. NPK 20-20-20 first 4 weeks → switch to Potassium Sulphate or NPK 5-10-30 from fruit set. Potassium directly determines sugar content (Brix level) and market premium.

Harvest Indicators — How to Know a Watermelon is Ready

  • Tendril rule: The small tendril (curly vine) immediately adjacent to the fruit stem goes brown and dry when the melon is ripe. This is the most reliable indicator.
  • Ground spot: The patch where the melon rests on the ground turns from white to cream/pale yellow.
  • Sound test: Tap the melon with your knuckle — ripe melon gives a hollow "thump". Unripe gives a metallic "ping".
  • Bloom: The waxy white coating on the melon surface dulls slightly when ripe.
⚠ Never harvest early — a watermelon that is cut before ripeness has zero sweetness and will not ripen off-vine. One day too early = total loss of that fruit's value. Test 3 melons from different plants before calling the entire zone ready for harvest.

Post-Harvest

  • Handle gently — watermelons bruise internally without external signs. Internal bruising reduces shelf life from 3 weeks to 5–7 days.
  • Transport upright, single layer only. Never stack more than 2 high.
  • Shelf life: 2–3 weeks at room temperature. Do not refrigerate (chilling injury below 10°C).

Planting Protocol — Non-Negotiables

1

Only buy IITA-certified seed yams. Planting infected or poor-quality setts is the single biggest cause of yam crop failure in Nigeria. Spend the extra money. Poor setts from local markets carry yam mosaic virus, nematodes, and rot pathogens. Certified setts from IITA Ibadan or their affiliated seed systems.

2

Sett size: 300–450g. Below 250g = weak plants, poor yield. Above 500g = unnecessary expense as yield gain is marginal. Cut setts from healthy tubers using a clean knife; dust cut surfaces with wood ash or thiram fungicide immediately to prevent rot.

3

Planting mounds: Mounds must be at least 30–40cm high, 80–100cm diameter. This is critical — yam tubers grow downward from the sett. If there is not enough loose soil depth, tubers grow sideways and are small, deformed, and difficult to harvest.

4

Plant the sett on the upper slope of the mound, not the peak. Cover with 5–8cm of soil. Shallow planting leads to sett rotting in heat; deep planting delays emergence.

5

Stake every mound with a 2.5m bamboo stake within 2 weeks of planting (before vines start climbing). Unstaked yam yields 30–40% less than staked yam. This is the most consistently ignored and most consistently costly mistake on Nigerian yam farms.

6

Mulch at planting. Apply 5–10cm thick layer of dry grass, rice straw, or dry leaves over entire mound surface. Mulch retains soil moisture, reduces soil temperature, suppresses weeds, and improves tuber development. Remove mulch once vines cover the mound naturally.

The Storage Strategy — How to 2× Your Revenue

H

Harvest November–December when vines die back. Dig carefully from the non-sett end of the mound with a fork (not a cutlass — never cut tubers accidentally). Market price at this point: ₦350–₦450/kg.

H

Store in a yam barn — shade structure with airflow, 50–60cm off the ground on wooden frames. Each tuber must be kept separate, not touching. Inspect weekly — remove any rotting tubers immediately before rot spreads.

H

Sell in May–July when market price peaks at ₦500–₦700/kg. The same tuber that sells at ₦400/kg in December sells at ₦600/kg in June. Store losses of 10–15% are more than offset by the 50–70% price increase.

💡 Intercropping opportunity: In the first 4 months after planting (before yam canopy closes), plant cucumber or okra between yam mounds. These short-cycle crops earn additional income from the same 1,800 sqm while yam establishes. Remove intercrops before yam vines start spreading aggressively at Month 4.

Planting & Management

1

Sow 2 seeds per hole, 3–4cm deep. Thin to 1 at 10 days. Spacing: 75cm between rows, 30cm between plants.

2

Fertilise at knee height (V6 stage, approx 4 weeks) with urea side-dress (100 kg/ha). This is the most critical fertiliser timing for corn — the "knee-high by July 4th" principle — miss this window and yield is permanently reduced.

3

Fall Armyworm (FAW) — Nigeria's #1 corn pest. Apply Coragen (Chlorantraniliprole) 0.4ml/L directly into the leaf whorl at V3–V6 stage. FAW hides in the whorl during the day — spray must reach inside the funnel. Standard leaf spraying is ineffective against FAW.

Harvest & Market

H

Fresh sweet corn: Harvest 18–22 days after silk emergence, when kernels are tender and milky. Shelf life is extremely short — harvest-to-market within 24 hours is essential for quality. Night harvest + early morning Lagos delivery.

H

Roadside corn-on-cob sellers (Lagos Island, Victoria Island) pay ₦250–₦400 per cob. Retailers buy bundles. Hotels buy husk-on cobs for presentation — premium channel.

Crop Rotation — Why Zone H Exists

Rotation breaks the pest and disease cycles that accumulate when the same crop family is grown in the same soil repeatedly. This is one of the most undervalued practices in Nigerian smallholder farming — and one of the most profitable free interventions available.

Golden Rule: Never grow the same crop family in the same bed two consecutive seasons. Solanaceae (tomatoes, pepper) must not follow each other. Cucurbits (cucumber, watermelon, ugwu) must not follow each other. Alternating with legumes (cowpea, groundnut, beans) for one season restores soil nitrogen and breaks pest cycles — reducing pesticide costs by 30–40% in the following crop.

4.2 What Makes the Biggest Difference in Year 1

Management Quality (±40% yield impact)

The difference between 2.0 kg/sqm and 3.0 kg/sqm cucumber yield is not the seeds — it is whether the farm supervisor scouted for pests on time, applied fertiliser correctly, and harvested on schedule. Better management is free. It is the highest-ROI investment on any farm.

Buyer Relationships (±50% price impact)

A farmer who sells cucumber at ₦150/kg wholesale and a farmer who sells at ₦280/kg to a hotel are growing the same crop. The ₦130/kg difference is a relationship. Building 3–5 direct buyer contacts in Year 1 — hotels, supermarkets, restaurants — is worth more than any input cost saving.

Counter-Seasonal Farming (±100% price impact)

A borehole and pump (₦375,000 investment) enables dry-season farming when 90% of competition has no crop in the ground. Dry-season tomato at ₦500/kg vs ₦250/kg in season. This single infrastructure item more than doubles effective farm revenue without adding a single sqm of land.

4.3 The Greenhouse Decision — When and How Much

A 1,000 sqm greenhouse on Prymepoint genuinely changes the economics. Greenhouse cucumber: 12–16 kg/sqm/cycle (the figure I incorrectly applied to open field). 1,000 sqm × 14 kg/sqm × 6 cycles × ₦220/kg = ₦18,480,000/year from 1,000 sqm. That is 6× what the same 1,000 sqm produces open field. Cost to build: ₦8–12M (galvanised frame + UV polythene + drip irrigation). Payback: 6–8 months. The greenhouse is the single highest-ROI infrastructure investment available to Agrofini.
